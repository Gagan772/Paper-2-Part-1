## **Q11: What according to Joseph Nye are the major sources of country's soft power? Discuss its relevance in the contemporary world politics.** (2018, 10 Marks)

### üéØ **Core Theme**
Joseph Nye's soft power concept identifies three major sources‚Äîculture, political values, and foreign policies‚Äîenabling influence through attraction rather than coercion, increasingly relevant in globalized, interconnected world where military solutions prove costly.

### üîë **Key Recall Terms**
- **Soft Power**: Attraction over coercion (Joseph Nye)
- **Three Sources**: Culture, political values, foreign policies
- **Hard Power Contrast**: Military force and economic incentives
- **Cultural Appeal**: Hollywood, K-pop, education exports
- **Political Values**: Democracy, human rights, liberalism
- **Legitimate Foreign Policies**: Multilateralism, peacekeeping

### üìù **Answer Structure**

#### **Introduction**
- Joseph Nye coined "soft power" term
- In *Bound to Lead: The Changing Nature of American Power* (1990)
- Defines as:
  - Ability to shape others' preferences
  - Through attraction rather than coercion or payment
- Contrasts with hard power:
  - Hard power relies on military force
  - Hard power relies on economic incentives
- Nye identifies three major sources of soft power:
  - Culture
  - Political values
  - Foreign policies
- These elements enable nation to influence:
  - Global attitudes
  - Behaviors
  - Fostering admiration and shared identity

#### **Body**

*First Source - Culture:*
- Refers to country's cultural appeal:
  - Arts
  - Education
  - Entertainment
  - Language
- **Example**: Hollywood films
  - American music
  - Educational institutions like Harvard and Stanford
  - Significant role in projecting U.S. soft power worldwide
- Nye argues:
  - Culture resonating with foreign audiences
  - Creates favorable environment for:
    - Diplomatic relationships
    - Economic relationships
- Contemporary examples:
  - South Korea effectively uses cultural exports
  - K-pop and Korean cinema
  - Build global influence
  - Phenomenon known as "Korean Wave"
- Cultural diplomacy creates:
  - Positive associations
  - Enhances nation's global standing

*Second Source - Political Values:*
- When country's domestic policies align with:
  - Widely accepted international norms
  - Democracy
  - Human rights
  - Individual freedoms
- Can enhance global standing
- Nye argues:
  - "Values of democracy, liberalism, free markets"
  - "Attractive to many people around world"
- Countries upholding these values:
  - Scandinavian nations
  - Often rank high in global influence
  - Often rank high in respect
  - Contributing to soft power appeal
- Legitimacy of political system matters:
  - States perceived as just
  - States perceived as fair
  - Gain moral authority in international affairs

*Third Source - Foreign Policies:*
- Emphasizes how country conducts itself internationally
- Nye notes:
  - Policies perceived as legitimate
  - Policies perceived as morally sound
  - Increase nation's soft power
- **Example**: Canada's commitment to:
  - Peacekeeping missions
  - Promotion of multilateralism
  - Enhanced global reputation
- Contrast - actions undermining soft power:
  - Coercive policies
  - Unilateral actions
- **Example**: U.S. invasion of Iraq (2003)
  - Seen as unilateral
  - Undermined U.S. soft power globally

*Contemporary Relevance:*
- Increasingly relevant in contemporary world
- Globalization deepens interdependence
- Non-state actors gain influence
- Era where military solutions:
  - Often prove costly
  - Often prove ineffective
- Countries focus on expanding soft power:
  - China through diplomacy
  - India through cultural exchange
  - International development initiatives
- Soft power complements traditional forms:
  - Crucial tool in shaping global politics
  - Achieving foreign policy goals
  - Without resorting to force

#### **Conclusion**
- Soft power crucial instrument in contemporary world politics
- Three sources (culture, values, policies):
  - Enable states to influence others
  - Through attraction and persuasion
  - Rather than coercion
- In interconnected world:
  - Soft power increasingly important
  - Complements hard power
  - In achieving strategic objectives

2. **Korean Wave (Hallyu)**: K-pop, Korean cinema global cultural diplomacy
3. **Scandinavian Nations**: Democracy, human rights enhancing global standing
4. **Canada's Peacekeeping**: Multilateralism boosting international reputation
5. **U.S. Iraq Invasion (2003)**: Unilateral action undermining soft power

### üéØ **Core Theme**

### üîë **Key Recall Terms**

  - *Transnational Relations and World Politics* (1971)

    - States
    - Non-state actors
  - Led to complex interactions transcending borders
  - Diminishing centrality of state
- MNCs play significant role:
  - Shaping economic policies
  - Influencing state decisions

  - Google
  - Apple
  - ExxonMobil

- Gives substantial bargaining power in global politics

  - Pressuring governments

  - Human rights

  - Amnesty International

- Lead to reforms

  - Environmental NGOs pressured states
  - Adopt more sustainable practices
- Demonstrates civil society power:
  - To shape policy
  - To hold governments accountable

*Terrorist Organizations - Security Challenge:*
- Represent another category of transnational actors
- Disrupted state sovereignty
- **Examples**: Groups like:
  - Al-Qaeda
  - ISIS
- Operate across borders
- Challenge state's monopoly on violence
- Compelling governments to:
  - Collaborate internationally
  - Address security threats
- Demonstrates non-state armed actors:
  - Can undermine state authority
  - Operate beyond state control

*International Organizations - Governance Challenge:*
- Further diminished state sovereignty
- By establishing:
  - Rules states must follow
  - Norms states must follow
- **Examples**: Organizations like:
  - United Nations
  - World Trade Organization
  - International Monetary Fund
- Set international standards
- **Prime Example**: European Union
  - Supranational governance
  - Can limit state autonomy
  - Member states cede decision-making powers
  - To central authority
- Demonstrates erosion of:
  - Traditional absolute sovereignty

#### **Conclusion**
- Modern state no longer sole actor in international relations
- Transnational actors significantly impacted:
  - State sovereignty
  - Governance
  - Policy-making
- Categories of transnational actors:
  - MNCs (economic power)
  - NGOs (advocacy power)
  - Terrorist groups (security challenges)
  - International organizations (governance rules)
- Changing nature reflects:
  - More interconnected global order
  - More complex global order
  - Power diffused across multiple actors
  - Beyond traditional state apparatus

<a name="q15-conceptual-dimensions-of-collective-security"></a>
## **Q15: Discuss the conceptual dimensions of collective security.** (2022, 15 Marks)

### üéØ **Core Theme**
Collective security operates on principle that attack on one member constitutes attack on all, requiring collective response through dimensions of universality, deterrence, consensus, and equality, though practical challenges like veto power and conflicting interests limit effectiveness.

### üîë **Key Recall Terms**
- **Collective Security**: Attack on one is attack on all
- **Universality**: All states participate in security system
- **Deterrence**: Fear of collective retaliation prevents aggression
- **Consensus Challenge**: Agreeing on threats and responses
- **Equality Principle**: All states have equal security right
- **UN Security Council**: P5 veto power contradicts equality

### üìù **Answer Structure**

#### **Introduction**
- Collective security concept:
  - System where states agree to work together
  - Prevent and respond to threats
  - Against peace and security
- Central idea:
  - Attack on one member
  - Considered attack on all
  - Prompting collective response
  - To restore peace
- Principle represents shift:
  - From traditional balance of power politics
  - Where states focused on self-interest
  - Where states formed shifting alliances
- To cooperative approach:
  - Aimed at maintaining global or regional security

#### **Body**

*Dimension 1 - Universality:*
- Key conceptual dimension
- Idea that all states should participate:
  - In collective security system
- Principle rooted in belief:
  - Security is indivisible
  - Threat to one nation is threat to all
- For collective security to be effective:
  - All members of international system must commit
  - To enforcement of peace
  - Regardless of direct interest in particular conflict
- **Example**: United Nations
  - Most prominent institution built on this idea
  - Chapter VII of UN Charter:
    - Allows UN Security Council
    - To take collective action
    - Including use of force
    - To address threats to international peace

*Dimension 2 - Deterrence:*
- Collective security designed to deter potential aggressors
- By making clear:
  - Any act of aggression
  - Will be met with overwhelming opposition
- Assumption:
  - Fear of collective retaliation
  - Will dissuade states from aggressive behavior
- **Example**: Korean War (1950)
  - United Nations intervened militarily
  - In response to North Korea's invasion
  - Of South Korea
  - Demonstrating collective security framework in action

*Dimension 3 - Consensus Challenge:*
- Faces significant challenges
- Particularly in terms of:
  - Commitment
  - Consensus
- Effective collective security requires:
  - All member states agree on:
    - What constitutes threat
    - How to respond
- Often proves difficult in practice
- Especially when interests of major powers conflict
- **Example**: During Cold War
  - Bipolar rivalry between U.S. and Soviet Union
  - Often paralyzed UN Security Council
  - Superpowers used veto power
  - To block collective action
  - Undermining efficacy of collective security

*Dimension 4 - Equality Principle vs. Reality:*
- Another key dimension
- Though challenged by realities of international politics
- In theory:
  - Collective security rests on principle
  - All states have equal right to security
  - Regardless of size or power
- In practice:
  - Influence of major powers distorts principle
- **Example**: UN Security Council
  - Unequal distribution of power
  - Five permanent members (P5) hold veto power:
    - United States
    - Russia
    - China
    - France
    - United Kingdom
  - Contradicts egalitarian ideal

*Contemporary Examples:*
- Despite challenges:
  - Collective security remains essential framework
  - For international peacekeeping
  - For conflict resolution
- **Example**: North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO)
  - Operates on collective security principle
  - Article 5 of NATO Treaty:
    - Armed attack against one member
    - Is attack against all
  - Invoked for first time:
    - After September 11, 2001 attacks
    - On United States

#### **Conclusion**
