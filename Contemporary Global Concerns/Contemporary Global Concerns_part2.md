## <a id="q11"></a>Q11. Examine the main problems and challenges involved in looking after environmental concerns in world politics. (2016) (20 Marks)

### üéØ Core Theme
Environmental concerns in world politics face challenges from conflicting national interests, sovereignty constraints, economic development tensions, resource inequalities, and weak enforcement mechanisms, requiring coordinated global efforts balancing economic growth with sustainability.

### üîë Key Recall Terms
Economic development vs environment | Arthur Pigou | Polluter Pays Principle | Robert Keohane | Complex interdependence | Green Climate Fund | Sovereignty concerns | Resource inequality | Climate-induced displacement | Technology transfer | Sahel conflicts

### üìù Answer Structure

**Introduction:**
- Challenges due to conflicting national interests
- Economic priorities versus environmental protection
- Complex nature of global governance
- Tension between development and conservation

**Body:**

**Economic Development vs Environmental Protection:**
- Developing countries: strict regulations inhibit economic growth
- Rely heavily on natural resource exploitation
- Arthur Pigou: "Polluter Pays Principle"
- Those responsible for pollution bear management costs
- Developing nations: historical responsibility lies with industrialized nations
- Creates significant divide
- Evident in Kyoto Protocol (1997) and Paris Agreement (2015)

**Global Cooperation Challenges:**
- International system fundamentally state-centric
- Sovereignty trumps collective environmental action
- Robert Keohane's "complex interdependence" theory
- Global environmental issues require transnational cooperation
- Sovereignty concerns and differing national interests
- Make consensus difficult
- U.S. withdrawal from Paris Agreement (2017)
- National political shifts undermine global initiatives
- Shows fragility of multilateral agreements

**Resource and Technology Inequality:**
- Unequal distribution of resources and technological capabilities
- Developed nations: financial and technological resources for green policies
- Developing countries: inadequate infrastructure and capacity
- Green Climate Fund established 2010
- Aimed to support developing nations
- Promised $100 billion per year remains underfunded
- Challenges in mobilizing financial support
- Amartya Sen: without addressing inequalities, policies insufficient and inequitable

**Interconnected Broader Issues:**
- Environmental concerns linked to poverty, migration, geopolitical conflicts
- Climate-induced displacement: growing crisis
- Lacks legal framework under international law
- Millions left vulnerable
- Competition for scarce resources: water and arable land
- Exacerbates conflicts
- Sahel region: environmental degradation intensified disputes and migration

**Conclusion:**
- Deeply entangled with economic interests, sovereignty, resource inequalities
- Despite frameworks like Paris Agreement, real progress requires:
  - Greater financial commitment
  - Technological transfers
  - Rethinking development paradigms
- Balance economic growth with sustainability
- Without coordinated efforts, governance remains challenging

**Competition in Green Energy:**

**Conclusion:**
- Approaches differ significantly
- China: centralized, investment-driven strategy

- Efforts to balance growth with sustainability

**Body:**

**Tragedy of the Commons:**
- Garrett Hardin's concept
- Individuals or states acting in self-interest
- Overuse and deplete shared resources
- Leads to long-term damage
- Overexploitation of fisheries in international waters
- Declining fish stocks
- Threatens global food security and marine biodiversity
- Greenhouse gas emissions contribute to global warming
- Impacts global climate system
- Safeguarding requires collective action and responsibility
- No single nation can effectively manage alone

**Global Governance Mechanisms:**
- International treaties established
- Address challenges of managing global commons
- United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS)
- Governs use of world's oceans
- Guidelines for maritime conduct
- Conservation of marine resources
- Montreal Protocol (1987)
- Successful example of global cooperation
- Protecting atmosphere
- Phasing out ozone-depleting substances
- Significant recovery of ozone layer
- Demonstrates effective coordinated global action

**Challenges in Management:**
- Lack of enforcement mechanisms
- Competing interests of states
- Climate change remains critical issue
- Paris Agreement: shows potential and limitations
- Relies on voluntary national commitments
- Absence of binding enforcement
- Difficult to hold countries accountable
- Arctic ice cap melting
- Competition among states for resources
- Oil and shipping routes
- Complicates cooperative management efforts

**Conclusion:**
- Global commons belong to global society
- Necessitate global attention
- Effective management requires:
  - Robust international cooperation
  - Legal frameworks
  - Enforcement mechanisms
- Progress through various global agreements
- Sustainable management remains complex challenge
- Need stronger multilateral action and accountability
- Protect shared spaces for future generations

## <a id="q16"></a>Q16. Do you endorse that the conventional discourse on human rights has failed to include women's rights? Explain in the context of feminist theories. (2015) (20 Marks)

### üéØ Core Theme

### üìù Answer Structure

**Introduction:**
- Feminist theorists critique conventional discourse
- Failing to adequately include women's rights
- Traditional frameworks: UDHR (1948)
- Gender-neutral approach assumption
- All individuals have same experiences and access
- Feminist scholars: overlooks specific discrimination
- Violence and inequality women face
- Marginalizes women's rights within broader narrative

**Body:**

**Public vs Private Sphere Neglect:**
- Catharine MacKinnon's critique
- Human rights discourse prioritizes public and political rights
- Freedom of speech, right to vote, protection from detention
- Neglects private sphere issues
- Disproportionately affect women
- Gender-based violence: domestic abuse, sexual assault, reproductive rights
- Occur in private spaces
- Rooted in societal power imbalances
- Issues not merely personal but systemic
- Reflect patriarchal structures
- Remain unchallenged in traditional frameworks

**Male-Centric Framework:**
- Hilary Charlesworth: conventional discourse "male-centric"
- International human rights law developed by male-dominated institutions
- Don't fully understand or prioritize women's specific needs
- Absence of women in decision-making positions
- Leads to lack of focus on:
  - Maternal health
  - Equal pay
  - Right to live free from gender-based violence
- Crucial for realization of women's rights

**CEDAW and Implementation Weaknesses:**
- Convention on Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women (1979)
- Significant step toward addressing gap
- Feminist scholars: implementation remains weak
- Many countries fail to ratify
- Attach reservations limiting effectiveness
- CEDAW calls for equal rights in marriage and family
- Countries uphold discriminatory practices
- Under guise of cultural and religious norms
- Shows limitations in protecting women's rights

**Intersectionality Concerns:**
- Postcolonial theorists: Chandra Talpade Mohanty
- Need for intersectionality in human rights discourse
- Women's rights cannot be understood in isolation
- Must examine intersections: race, class, sexuality, colonial history
- Conventional discourse fails to address diverse forms of oppression
- Many women, especially in marginalized communities, experience

**Conclusion:**
- Conventional discourse failed to adequately incorporate women's rights
- Neglects private sphere
- Lacks gender sensitivity
- Ignores intersectional issues
- Feminist theories call for:
  - More inclusive approach
  - Transformative approach
  - Recognizes women's unique experiences
  - Integrates their rights into broader framework
