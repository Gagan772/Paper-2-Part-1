## <a id="q1"></a>Q1. In what ways does the functionalist approach in International relations help in maintaining peace and order in global politics? (2023) (15 Marks)

### ğŸ¯ Core Theme

### ğŸ“ Answer Structure

**Introduction:**
- Functionalism emphasizes cooperation through non-political, technical, and economic institutions
- Developed by David Mitrany as alternative to power politics
- Focuses on shared interests in trade, health, environmental management
- Promotes interdependence among states to reduce conflict likelihood
- Enhances global stability through practical cooperation

**Body:**

*Mitrany's Theoretical Framework:*
- *A Working Peace System* (1943) - seminal work
- Cooperation in technical/economic spheres leads to political cooperation
- Nations working on common problems develop cooperation habits
- Problems: poverty, disease, climate change require collective action
- Conflict becomes less likely through functional integration
- Peace achieved incrementally, not through grand political agreements

*Role of International Institutions:*
- WHO addresses global health challenges
- IMF manages international financial stability
- Organizations solve problems no single state can address alone
- Foster peaceful relations through technical expertise
- Create networks of cooperation transcending political boundaries

*European Integration Example:*
- ECSC (1951) linked coal and steel production
- Economic cooperation between France and Germany
- Former rivals became integrated partners
- Evolved into broader political and economic union (EU)
- Reduced likelihood of war in Europe
- Demonstrates economic integration leading to political unity
- Validates Mitrany's vision of functional cooperation

*Global Climate Action:*
- Paris Agreement (2015) exemplifies functional cooperation
- States collaborate despite political differences
- Common challenge transcends national politics
- Fosters dialogue and reduces tensions
- Emphasizes shared goals over divisive politics
- Demonstrates transnational cooperation on functional issues

*Mechanisms of Peace Maintenance:*
- Creates mutual dependencies between states
- Builds trust through repeated cooperation
- Technical issues less contentious than political ones
- Expert-driven cooperation bypasses political conflicts
- Gradual spillover from technical to political integration

**Conclusion:**
- Functionalism maintains peace through non-political cooperation
- Creates interdependence and mutual trust among states
- Addresses shared technical/economic challenges
- Mitigates conflicts through practical problem-solving
- Contributes to long-term global stability
- Examples: EU integration, international environmental agreements

## <a id="q2"></a>Q2. What do you mean by offensive and defensive realism? (2023) (15 Marks)

### ğŸ¯ Core Theme
Offensive and defensive realism represent two contrasting strategies within realist IR theory: offensive realism advocates aggressive power maximization for survival, while defensive realism promotes cautious balancing to avoid provoking counterbalancing threats.

### ğŸ”‘ Key Recall Terms
`John Mearsheimer` | `Kenneth Waltz` | `Tragedy of Great Power Politics` | `Structural Realism` | `Power Maximization` | `Security Maximization` | `Hegemony` | `Balance of Power` | `Anarchy` | `Self-Help` | `NATO` | `Peaceful Rise`

### ğŸ“ Answer Structure

**Introduction:**
- Two distinct strands within realist school
- Both focus on power and security
- Differ in explanations of state behavior
- Rooted in anarchic international system framework
- No overarching authority exists to enforce rules
- States rely on self-help strategies
- Diverge on power accumulation approaches

**Body:**

*Offensive Realism - Mearsheimer's Framework:*
- Articulated in *The Tragedy of Great Power Politics* (2001)
- Anarchic system requires aggressive power strategies
- Primary goal: achieve hegemony for survival
- States never satisfied with status quo
- Constantly seek relative power over rivals
- More power = reduced threat likelihood
- "The best defense is a good offense"
- Prepared to expand influence (military, economic, diplomatic)
- Nazi Germany's 1930s expansion as example
- U.S. global dominance post-WWII
- Power maximization ensures security

*Defensive Realism - Waltz's Framework:*
- Associated with Kenneth Waltz
- *Theory of International Politics* (1979)
- States seek power primarily for security
- Not necessarily to maximize beyond necessary
- International system compels security concerns
- Excessive power accumulation provokes balancing
- Leads to greater insecurity
- System punishes hegemonic pursuit
- Triggers counterbalancing coalitions
- Cold War example: Soviet expansion â†’ NATO
- Advocate cautious approach
- Emphasize balancing over expanding
- China's "peaceful rise" strategy exemplifies this
- Gradually increase power without alarming rivals

*Comparative Analysis:*
- Both emphasize power importance
- Offensive: maximizing power leads to security
- Defensive: warns against overreach
- State behavior reflects mix of both
- U.S. foreign policy oscillates between approaches
- Iraq invasion 2003 (offensive)
- Alliance maintenance (defensive)
- Context determines strategy adoption

**Conclusion:**
- Contrasting lenses for state behavior
- Offensive advocates power maximization
- Defensive stresses maintaining balance
- Both explain different historical actions
- Help understand contemporary global politics
- Applicable to various state strategies

## <a id="q4"></a>Q4. Discuss the emergence of neo-realism and its basic tenets (2021) (15 Marks)

### ğŸ¯ Core Theme
Neo-realism (structural realism) emerged as Kenneth Waltz's response to classical realism, shifting focus from human nature to the structure of the anarchic international system as the primary determinant of state behavior and power dynamics.

### ğŸ”‘ Key Recall Terms
`Kenneth Waltz` | `Structural Realism` | `Anarchy` | `Balance of Power` | `State Rationality` | `Unitary Actors` | `Relative Gains` | `Security Dilemma` | `Bipolarity` | `System Structure` | `Capabilities Distribution` | `Gulf War`

### ğŸ“ Answer Structure

**Introduction:**
- Emerged late 20th century
- Response to classical realism limitations
- Associated with Kenneth Waltz
- *Theory of International Politics* (1979)
- Redefined theoretical landscape
- Structure of international system key determinant
- Not human nature or domestic factors
- Emphasizes systemic constraints on states

**Body:**

*Central Concept - Anarchic Structure:*
- International system is anarchic
- No overarching authority to enforce rules
- No order maintenance among states
- Compels states to prioritize survival and security
- Act in power-centric ways
- Waltz: "structure of international system is central influence"
- Shapes distribution of capabilities
- Determines state interactions

*Balance of Power Tenet:*
- States motivated to maintain security
- Seek to balance against powerful states
- Internal balancing: increase military capabilities
- External balancing: form alliances
- Cold War exemplifies this principle
- US-Soviet containment strategies
- Alliance-building to counter influence
- Bipolar world order
- Military and ideological competition

*State Rationality and Unitary Actors:*
- States act as rational entities
- Decisions based on national interest calculations
- Decisions based on power calculations
- Simplifies domestic politics complexities
- Focus on state interactions
- Pursuit of security objectives
- Gulf War (1990-1991) example
- States formed coalitions to counter Iraq
- Collective security concerns
- Neo-realist lens explains coalition formation

*Relative Gains Concept:*
- States concerned with comparative power
- How power/security compares to others
- More important than absolute gains
- Leads to security dilemmas
- Actions to enhance security threaten others
- Prompts arms race or tension escalation
- Nuclear proliferation in South Asia example
- Zero-sum thinking in international relations

*Systemic Pressures:*
- Structure constrains state choices
- Polarity matters (unipolar, bipolar, multipolar)
- Distribution of capabilities shapes outcomes
- States respond to systemic imperatives
- Survival is paramount concern

**Conclusion:**
- Significantly shaped IR field
- Structured analysis of state behavior
- Grounded in anarchic system nature
- Focus on power dynamics
- State rationality emphasis
- Importance of balancing strategies
- Compelling framework for understanding global politics
- Applicable to rapidly changing world

## <a id="q5"></a>Q5. Bring out the major differences between the classical Realism of Hans Morgenthau and Neorealism of Kenneth Waltz. (2018) (10 Marks)

### ğŸ¯ Core Theme
Classical Realism roots state behavior in flawed human nature and will to power, while Neorealism attributes it to anarchic system structureâ€”representing a fundamental shift from human-centric to structure-centric IR analysis.

### ğŸ”‘ Key Recall Terms
`Human Nature vs Structure` | `Will to Power` | `Anarchy` | `Morgenthau` | `Waltz` | `Power Politics` | `Security` | `Morality` | `Scientific Approach` | `Cold War` | `System Polarity`

### ğŸ“ Answer Structure

**Introduction:**
- Two significant schools of thought
- Morgenthau's *Politics Among Nations* (1948)
- Waltz's *Theory of International Politics* (1979)
- Both share foundational beliefs
- Pursuit of power drives politics
- International system is anarchic
- Differ in focus, assumptions, analytical approaches

**Body:**

*Understanding of Human Nature:*
- **Classical Realism:**
  - Emphasizes flawed, selfish human nature
  - "Will to power" rooted in human nature
  - Leaders act from desire to dominate
  - Conflicts arise from lust for power
  - Philosophical view of power politics
  - Morgenthau: "Politics governed by objective laws rooted in human nature"
  
- **Neorealism:**
  - Shifts focus from human nature
  - Focus on structure of international system
  - Not inherent human characteristics drive behavior
  - Anarchic nature of system is key
  - Lack of central authority compels self-help
  - Prioritizing survival and security
  - Distribution of power determines behavior
  - Unipolar, bipolar, or multipolar system matters

*Approach to Power:*
- **Classical Realism:**
  - Power in military capabilities
  - Political influence primary
  - Pursuit of power is main goal
  - Leads to competition and conflict
  
- **Neorealism:**
  - Power in structural terms
  - Power is means to achieve security
  - Not an end in itself
  - States seek to balance power
  - Balance as stabilizing force
  - System equilibrium important

*Role of Morality:*
- **Classical Realism:**
  - Focuses on moral/ethical dimensions
  - Acknowledges role of ethics
  - Skeptical about influence given power realities
  - Moral considerations vs power politics tension
  
- **Neorealism:**
  - More scientific and structural
  - Dismisses role of morality in anarchy
  - Establishes rigorous, objective analysis
  - Focus on empirical patterns
  - Seeks scientific explanations

*Historical Application:*
- Cold War interpretation differs
- Morgenthau: US-Soviet rivalry from leaders' dominance desire
- Waltz: Bipolar structure made conflict inevitable
- Different explanatory frameworks
- Same events, different causes

**Conclusion:**
- Share core assumptions about power and anarchy
- Differ in what drives state behavior
- Morgenthau: human nature and power politics
- Waltz: system structure and quest for security
- Both contribute to understanding IR
- Complementary perspectives

- Suitability as "best" method debated

**Body:**

*Strengths of Classical Realism:*
- **Inherently Conflictual Nature:**
  - States are rational actors
  - Primarily concerned with survival and security
  - Morgenthau's *Politics Among Nations* (1948)
  - Power is primary currency
  - Moral considerations subordinate to national interest
  
- **Cold War Explanation:**
  - Explained superpower behavior

  - Emphasis on anarchy in system

  - Overlooks domestic politics role
  - Ideology in shaping foreign policy
  - Assumes states act as unitary, rational actors
  - Graham Allison's Bureaucratic Politics Model
  - Internal politics influence decisions
  - Cuban Missile Crisis example
  - Bureaucratic struggles matter
  
- **Cannot Explain Non-State Actors:**
  - Struggles with rise of non-state actors
  - Transnational threats inadequately addressed
  - Terrorism, climate change, global health crises
  - Require multilateral cooperation
  - Need diplomacy and international governance
  - State-centric focus too narrow
  
- **Overemphasis on Conflict:**
  - Cooperation instances underexplained
  - European integration puzzling from realist view
  - Economic interdependence growth
  - International law development
  - Human rights norms emergence
  
- **Static View of Interests:**
  - National interest assumed fixed
  - Doesn't account for changing values
  - Ideological shifts matter
  - Constructivist critique relevant

**Conclusion:**
- Essential method for understanding IR
- Especially regarding power dynamics and conflict
- Not necessarily "best" method comprehensively
- Cannot explain all complexities
- Today's global system too multifaceted
- Focus on state behavior provides valuable insights
- Limitations in addressing cooperation
- Non-state actors inadequately covered
- Transnational issues need other frameworks
- Liberalism and Constructivism also necessary
- Fully understand evolving landscape
- Complementary approaches needed

## Q.2) What do you mean by offensive and defensive realism? (2023) (15 Marks)

### Approach to write the answer

**Introduction:**  
â€¢ Introduce offensive and defensive realism as two strands within the realist school of international relations, focusing on power and security in the anarchic international system.

**Body:**  
â€¢ Explain that offensive realism emphasizes aggressive power maximization for survival, while defensive realism advocates for cautious power accumulation to avoid provoking counterbalancing threats.

**Conclusion:**  
â€¢ Highlight that both theories offer contrasting views on state behavior, with offensive realism promoting power expansion and defensive realism warning against overreach, explaining various state actions.

### Answer

Offensive and defensive realism are two distinct strands within the realist school of thought in international relations, both of which focus on the role of power and security but differ in their explanations of state behavior. Both derive from the broader framework of realism, which emphasizes the anarchic structure of the international system, where no overarching authority exists to enforce rules or protect states, compelling them to rely on self-help strategies. However, offensive and defensive realism diverge in their perspectives on how states should approach power accumulation and security.

Offensive realism, as articulated by John Mearsheimer in *The Tragedy of Great Power Politics* (2001), argues that in an anarchic international system, states must pursue aggressive strategies to maximize their power. According to Mearsheimer, the primary goal of states is to achieve hegemony, as this is the best way to ensure their survival. Offensive realism posits that states are never satisfied with the status quo; they constantly seek to gain relative power over their rivals, as more power reduces the likelihood of being threatened. Mearsheimer famously argues, "The best defense is a good offense," suggesting that states must be prepared to expand their influence, whether through military, economic, or diplomatic means. Historical examples, such as Nazi Germany's aggressive expansion in the 1930s or the U.S.'s global dominance after World War II, are often cited as evidence of offensive realism in practice.

In contrast, defensive realism, primarily associated with Kenneth Waltz and his work *Theory of International Politics* (1979), contends that states seek power primarily to ensure their security, not necessarily to maximize it beyond what is necessary. Defensive realists argue that while the international system compels states to be concerned with security, excessive power accumulation can provoke balancing behavior from other states, leading to greater insecurity. Both theories highlight the importance of power, but while offensive realism argues that maximizing power leads to security, defensive realism warns against overreach. Offensive and defensive realism provide contrasting lenses through which to view state behavior in the anarchic international system. Neo-realism, also known as structural realism, emerged in the late 20th century as a response to the limitations of classical realism in international relations theory. Primarily associated with the works of Kenneth Waltz, particularly his 1979 book *Theory of International Politics*, neo-realism redefined the theoretical landscape by emphasizing the structure of the international system as the key determinant of state behavior rather than human nature or domestic factors.

At the heart of neo-realism is the notion that the international system is anarchic, meaning there is no overarching authority to enforce rules or maintain order among states. This anarchic structure compels states to prioritize their survival and security, leading them to act in ways that can be understood through a power-centric lens. Waltz argues that "the structure of the international system is the central influence on state behavior," asserting that it shapes the distribution of capabilities among states and subsequently determines their interactions.

One of the basic tenets of neo-realism is the concept of the balance of power. This can manifest through internal balancing (increasing military capabilities) or external balancing (forming alliances). The Cold War period exemplifies this principle, as the United States and the Soviet Union engaged in a strategy of containment and alliance-building to counter each other's influence, leading to a bipolar world order characterized by military and ideological competition.

Neo-realists assume that states act as rational entities, making decisions based on calculations of national interest and power. This perspective simplifies the complexities of domestic politics and focuses on how states interact in pursuit of their security objectives. For example, the decisions made by nations during the Gulf War in 1990-1991 can be analyzed through a neo-realist lens, where states formed coalitions to counter Iraq's aggression based on collective security concerns.

Furthermore, neo-realism introduces the concept of relative gains, emphasizing that states are more concerned about how their power and security compare to others rather than absolute gains. This perspective often leads to security dilemmas, where the actions taken by one state to enhance its security inadvertently threaten others, prompting an arms race or escalation of tensions, as seen in the nuclear proliferation debates in South Asia.

Neo-realism has significantly shaped the field of international relations by offering a structured analysis of state behavior grounded in the anarchic nature of the international system. By focusing on power dynamics, state rationality, and the importance of balancing strategies, neo-realism provides a compelling framework for understanding the complexities of global politics in a rapidly changing world.

While both Classical Realism and Neorealism share core assumptions about power and anarchy, they differ in their understanding of what drives state behaviorâ€”Morgenthau focuses on human nature and power politics, while Waltz emphasizes the structure of the international system and the quest for security.

Realism, particularly classical Realism, has long been regarded as one of the most influential frameworks for understanding international relations (IR). Rooted in the writings of thinkers like Thucydides, Thomas Hobbes, and later Hans Morgenthau, Realism is based on the premise that international relations are driven primarily by the pursuit of power, self-interest, and survival in an anarchic global system. While Realism has been foundational in shaping IR theory, its suitability as the "best" method for understanding global politics is debated, especially when applied to the complexities of the modern world.

Classical Realism emphasizes the inherently conflictual nature of international relations. It posits that states, as rational actors, are primarily concerned with their own survival and security. As Hans Morgenthau outlines in *Politics Among Nations* (1948), power is the primary currency in international relations, and moral considerations are often subordinate to the pursuit of national interest. Realism also struggles to account for the rise of non-state actors and transnational threats, such as terrorism, climate change, and global health crises. These challenges require multilateral cooperation, diplomacy, and international governanceâ€”factors that Realism, with its focus on state-centric power politics, tends to underestimate.

While classical Realism remains an essential method for understanding international relations, especially in terms of power dynamics and conflict, it is not necessarily the "best" method for comprehensively explaining the complexities of today's global system. Its focus on state behavior and power struggles provides valuable insights, but its limitations in addressing international cooperation, non-state actors, and transnational issues suggest that other frameworks, such as Liberalism or Constructivism, are also necessary to fully understand the evolving landscape of international relations.
