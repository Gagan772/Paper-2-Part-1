## <a id="q1"></a>Q1. Identify and evaluate the reasons for deadlock in the WTO negotiations on fisheries between the developing and developed countries. (2022) (20 Marks)

### ğŸ¯ Core Theme
The WTO fisheries subsidies deadlock reflects conflicting priorities between developed nations (advocating strict conservation measures) and developing countries (defending livelihood security and economic survival). The impasse stems from historical responsibility debates, definitional disputes over "harmful subsidies," and demands for special and differential treatment.

### ğŸ”‘ Key Recall Terms
`WTO Fisheries Deadlock` | `Subsidy Negotiations` | `Developed vs Developing` | `Marine Resource Depletion` | `Economic Dependency` | `Overfishing` | `Small-Scale Fishing` | `Livelihood Security` | `Food Security` | `Special and Differential Treatment` | `Harmful Subsidies` | `Historical Responsibility` | `Industrial Fishing Fleets` | `Common-Pool Resources` | `Globalization Paradox` | `Equity in Conservation`

### ğŸ“ Answer Structure

**Introduction**
- WTO fisheries subsidies negotiations highlight tension between developed and developing country interests
- Aim: limit subsidies leading to overfishing and marine resource depletion
- Stalled due to differing economic priorities, resource dependencies, and environmental responsibilities
- Developed countries push for stringent regulations to curb overfishing subsidies
- Developing nations argue restrictions threaten economic livelihoods and food security
- Small-scale fishing forms backbone of many developing economies

**Body**

*Economic Dependency of Developing Countries*
- Fisheries critical for livelihoods and food security in developing nations
- Dani Rodrik's "The Globalization Paradox" (2011): globalization pressures developing nations to adopt unsuitable standards
- Coastal and island nations (India, Indonesia, Philippines) heavily reliant on fisheries
- Fisheries provide income and employment to vulnerable coastal communities
- Strict subsidy limits disproportionately harm fishing communities in poorer nations
- Wealthier nations can transition away from subsidized fisheries more easily
- Small-scale fisheries lack capital for modernization
- Subsidies crucial for sustaining communities against competition from industrial fishing fleets
- Economic survival versus environmental conservation debate

*Imbalance of Responsibility in Marine Conservation*
- Developed countries advocate stricter controls with advanced technology and economies
- Developing countries cite historical contribution of developed nations to marine resource depletion
- Extensive industrial fishing by developed nations caused historical damage
- Elinor Ostrom's work on "Common-Pool Resources" (1990): effective management requires accounting for historical use and equity
- Burden of conservation should not fall equally on nations with different industrialization levels
- Unfair restriction on development prospects of emerging economies
- Historical justice demands differentiated responsibilities
- Equity principle in resource management

*Contested Definition and Scope of "Harmful Subsidies"*
- Developed countries identify fuel tax exemptions and fleet expansion funds as harmful
- Lead to overfishing and environmental degradation
- Developing nations argue not all subsidies are harmful
- Targeted support for small-scale fishers essential for poverty reduction
- WTO's "one-size-fits-all" approach fails to address unique needs of small-scale fisheries
- Subsistence fishing versus commercial fishing distinction
- Demand for "special and differential treatment" provisions
- Allow continuation of specific subsidies for small-scale and subsistence fishers
- Developed nations hesitant to support differentiated treatment
- Definitional ambiguity creates negotiation impasse

*Implementation and Compliance Concerns*
- Monitoring and enforcement capacity disparities
- Developing countries lack resources for complex compliance mechanisms
- Technology gap in tracking and reporting systems
- Transparency requirements may disadvantage nations with limited administrative capacity
- Need for capacity-building support from developed countries

**Conclusion**
- Deadlock reflects complex intersection of economic dependency, historical responsibility, and subsidy definitions
- Addressing impasse requires recognizing unique needs of developing countries
- Solution: allow differentiated responsibilities to balance conservation and development
- Aligns with sustainable development and equitable globalization principles
- Need for flexible framework accommodating diverse economic realities
- Path forward: special provisions for small-scale fishers while maintaining conservation goals

## <a id="q2"></a>Q2. Critically evaluate the role of the United States of America in the World Trade Organization (WTO) dispute settlement mechanism and its implications for the future of the WTO. (2020) (15 Marks)

### ğŸ¯ Core Theme
The USA's transformation from primary architect and supporter of the WTO dispute settlement mechanism to its most vocal critic reflects tensions between national sovereignty and multilateral trade governance. The US blockage of Appellate Body appointments has paralyzed the system, threatening the rules-based global trade order.

### ğŸ”‘ Key Recall Terms
`US-WTO Relations` | `Dispute Settlement Body (DSB)` | `Appellate Body Crisis` | `Judicial Overreach` | `Rules-Based Trade Order` | `Multilateralism` | `Unilateralism` | `2019 Appellate Body Blockage` | `Robert Lighthizer` | `Trade Conflict Resolution` | `Anti-Dumping Disputes` | `EU Banana Case` | `Credibility Crisis` | `Two-Track System` | `Trade Fragmentation`

### ğŸ“ Answer Structure

**Introduction**
- US played complex and influential role: architect and critic of WTO dispute settlement mechanism
- Established 1995: WTO's Dispute Settlement Body (DSB) integral to rules-based global trading order
- US traditionally strong supporter, relied on mechanism against major trading partners (China, EU)
- Recent criticism: Appellate Body accused of "judicial overreach"
- Rulings allegedly exceed WTO agreements' mandate
- Contentious stance has significant implications for WTO and multilateral trading system

**Body**

*Early US Support and Active Participation*
- US one of most frequent users in early years
- Joost Pauwelyn: US filed numerous cases shaping global trade norms
- Successfully challenged EU's banana import regime
- Secured favorable rulings upholding trade interests
- Reliance on WTO reflected belief in rules-based impartial system
- Key role in establishing mechanism's credibility
- Used DSB to address unfair trade practices globally
- Helped entrench WTO as legitimate arbiter of trade disputes

*Shift to Criticism and Opposition*
- Attitude changed after facing adverse rulings
- Issues: anti-dumping tariffs, subsidies, trade practices deemed WTO-inconsistent
- US Trade Representatives (Robert Lighthizer) criticized WTO for overstepping mandate
- Accusations of "activist" rulings beyond interpretive role
- 2019: US blocked appointment of new Appellate Body judges
- Paralyzed dispute resolution mechanism
- WTO data (2020): backlog of unresolved appeals
- Affected credibility and functionality of entire system
- Sovereignty concerns versus multilateral obligations

*Implications for WTO's Future*
- US blockage raised concerns about WTO viability
- Without functioning Appellate Body, disputes stall indefinitely
- Weakens enforcement of trade rules
- Countries may revert to unilateral measures
- Undermines multilateralism in global trade
- Simon Lester: risk of two-track system emerging
- Powerful countries resolve disputes outside WTO
- Marginalizes smaller economies in trade governance
- China may exploit weakened WTO for unchecked trade practices
- Destabilizes global trade norms
- Credibility crisis threatens WTO's central role

*Broader Systemic Concerns*
- Reflects US shift toward economic nationalism
- Preference for bilateral/regional agreements over multilateral frameworks
- Sets precedent for other nations to challenge WTO mechanisms
- Risk of trade order fragmentation
- Absence of neutral arbiter increases trade conflict likelihood
- Global economic uncertainty and protectionism may rise

**Conclusion**
- US played foundational role in WTO dispute settlement mechanism
- Recent actions destabilized the system
- Paralysis of Appellate Body casts uncertainty on future
- Threatens rules-based trade order
- Raises risk of unilateralism and trade fragmentation
- Restoring functionality requires negotiation and reform
- US remains key actor determining WTO's future trajectory
- Balance needed between national interests and multilateral trade governance

## <a id="q3"></a>Q3. Explain the significance and importance of the demand raised by the developing countries for a New International Economic Order (NIEO). Are they likely to achieve their objectives of NIEO in foreseeable future? (2020) (15 Marks)

### ğŸ¯ Core Theme
The NIEO movement of the 1970s represented developing countries' collective challenge to Western-dominated global economic structures, demanding fair trade terms, resource sovereignty, and structural reforms. ### ğŸ“ Answer Structure

**Introduction**
- NIEO emerged in 1970s: developing countries sought to restructure global economic system
- Address inequalities and improve economic standing
- Primarily advocated by Group of 77 (G-77) within United Nations
- Argument: existing economic order favored developed countries
- Perpetuated global disparities in wealth and resources
- Goals: fairer trade terms, increased development assistance, technology transfers
- Greater control over natural resources for developing nations

**Body**

*Significance: Challenge to Western Economic Dominance*
- NIEO challenged dominance of Western powers in global economy
- Samir Amin: existing structure marginalized developing countries
- Enforced dependence on raw material exports
- Developed countries controlled industrial production and trade terms
- Pushed for reforms to empower developing countries
- Control over resources and improved bargaining power in international trade
- Goal of "economic sovereignty" for formerly colonized nations
- Sought escape from "terms of trade trap"
- Raw material export prices stagnated relative to high-value imports
- Stabilized and improved prices for commodities demanded

*Importance: Development Finance and Debt Relief*
- Aimed to increase development assistance
- Ease debt burdens for sustainable growth
- Raul Prebisch: global imbalances exacerbated by lack of development finance
- Restrictive practices of IFIs (IMF, World Bank)
- Conditions often detrimental to developing economies
- Called for flexible, development-friendly financial assistance
- Allow nations to pursue independent growth trajectories
- Break cycle of debt dependency
- Enable self-sustaining development models

*Challenges to Achieving NIEO Objectives*
- Structural obstacles remain formidable
- Entrenched interests of developed nations
- Power imbalance in global institutions limits progress
- NIEO agenda waned by 1980s
- Developed countries favored market-oriented policies
- Promoted neoliberalism and privatization
- Conflicted with NIEO's state-led development goals
- Immanuel Wallerstein: capitalist world economy resists structural reform
- Difficult for developing nations to alter systemic position
- Core-periphery structure persists

*Alternative Frameworks and Current Prospects*
- BRICS initiatives (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa) renewed hope
- New Development Bank created for alternative financing
- South-South cooperation gaining momentum
- Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank (China-led)
- Provides alternatives to World Bank financing
- Global power structures still dominated by Western economies
- Voting power in IMF/World Bank unchanged substantially
- Limited progress in fundamental restructuring
- NIEO objectives unlikely fully realized in foreseeable future

**Conclusion**
- NIEO demand crucial in highlighting systemic inequalities
- Advocated for fairer global economic order
- Progress made in South-South cooperation and alternative frameworks
- Fundamental objectives unlikely fully realized soon
- Persistent dominance of developed nations in global economic governance
- Legacy: raised consciousness about structural inequalities
- Created precedent for developing world solidarity
- Contemporary relevance in debates on global economic justice

- Particularly strong in Asia
- Institutions provide emerging economies flexibility
- Emphasize infrastructure financing
- Fill gap Western-led institutions neglected
- Challenge monopoly of Bretton Woods institutions

*Advocacy for Inclusive Policies and Reform*
- Push for reforms within IMF and World Bank
- Address debt sustainability and poverty alleviation
- 2010 quota reform: modest victory for emerging economies
- Increased shares for China, India, Brazil
- Despite reform, push continues for deeper changes
- Enhance influence in decision-making
- Demand better representation in leadership positions
- Call for development-oriented policy frameworks
- Challenge neoliberal conditionalities

*Leveraging Economic Clout*
- China: world's second-largest economy
- Largest trading partner for many countries
- Ability to influence global financial rules and practices
- Belt and Road Initiative (BRI): expanded economic influence globally
- Alternative to Western-dominated financial assistance
- Reinforces ability to challenge US-Western financial dominance
- India: growing economic power with regional influence
- Brazil, Russia: resource-rich economies with strategic leverage
- Combined GDP of BRICS rivals Western economies

**Conclusion**
- Rising powers challenge US-Western dominance through multiple strategies
- Push for governance reforms in existing institutions
- Establish alternative financial institutions
- Use economic leverage to reshape global finance
- Reflect shifting balance of power in international relations
- Emerging economies seek greater role in shaping global financial rules
- Reflect new multipolar world order
- Transformation from unipolar Western dominance to multipolar system

## <a id="q5"></a>Q5. How far are the world governance mechanisms, dominated by IMF and world bank, legitimate and relevant? What measures do you suggest to improve their effectiveness in global governance? (2015) (15 Marks)

### ğŸ¯ Core Theme
IMF and World Bank face legitimacy crises due to Western-centric governance, neoliberal conditionalities, and inadequate representation of developing countries. While they remain relevant for crisis response, reforms toward inclusive governance, flexible policies, and transparency are essential for enhanced effectiveness.

### ğŸ”‘ Key Recall Terms
`IMF-World Bank Legitimacy` | `Bretton Woods 1944` | `Western-Centric Governance` | `Structural Adjustment Programs (SAPs)` | `Neoliberal Conditionalities` | `Voting Power Disparity` | `Joseph Stiglitz Critique` | `Amartya Sen` | `Sovereignty Erosion` | `Crisis Response` | `Governance Reform` | `Inclusive Representation` | `Developmental Flexibility` | `Transparency` | `Accountability`

### ğŸ“ Answer Structure

**Introduction**
- IMF and World Bank legitimacy and relevance debated
- Established 1944 at Bretton Woods Conference
- Created to promote global economic stability and development
- Criticisms grown, especially from developing countries
- Scholars argue institutions serve powerful economies over poorer nations
- Question: do they represent global interests or Western interests?

**Body**

*Legitimacy Crisis: Western Dominance*
- IMF and World Bank dominated by Western powers (especially US)
- Voting power based on financial contributions, not equal representation
- Disproportionate influence for developed nations (US, Europe)
- Joseph Stiglitz ("Globalization and Its Discontents"): IMF policies reflect major shareholders' priorities
- Imposed neoliberal economic policies unsuitable for developing countries
- Structural Adjustment Programs (SAPs) criticized heavily
- Prioritized fiscal austerity and market liberalization
- Led to social inequality and economic hardship
- Latin America and sub-Saharan Africa: 1980s-1990s devastation
- Legitimacy undermined by serving powerful nations' interests

*Issue of Conditionality and Sovereignty*
- Loans come with strict conditions
- Requirements: budget cuts, privatization, currency devaluation
- Led to economic instability in many developing nations
- Reduced public spending on healthcare and education
- Amartya Sen: one-size-fits-all policies undermine national sovereignty
- Impede long-term sustainable development
- Neglect social and political contexts of borrowing countries
- Conditionalities perceived as neocolonial control mechanisms
- Erode trust and legitimacy among recipient nations

*Continued Relevance Despite Criticism*
- Remain relevant for addressing global financial crises
- Provide financial assistance during emergencies
- Support development projects worldwide
- Expertise and resources valuable for global challenges
- Poverty reduction and economic stability missions important
- 2008 financial crisis: IMF played stabilization role
- COVID-19 pandemic: provided emergency financing
- Technical assistance and capacity building services
- Global coordination role in economic governance

*Suggested Reforms for Enhanced Effectiveness*
