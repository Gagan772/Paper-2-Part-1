## <a id="q11"></a>Q11. Do you endorse that the United Nations needs major changes in its structure and functioning? Suggest changes for efficient improvements. (2016) (15 Marks)

### ğŸ¯ Core Theme
The UN's 1945 post-WWII structure, centered on P5 Security Council dominance, fails to reflect contemporary geopolitical realities. Essential reforms include expanding UNSC membership to include emerging powers (India, Brazil, Germany, Japan), limiting veto use in mass atrocities cases, and enhancing administrative efficiency through transparency and streamlined peacekeeping operations.

### ğŸ”‘ Key Recall Terms
`UN Structural Reform Necessity` | `Post-WWII Framework Outdated` | `P5 Veto Power` | `Security Council Expansion` | `Emerging Powers Representation` | `India, Brazil, Germany, Japan (G4)` | `Administrative Efficiency` | `Bureaucratic Inefficiencies` | `Kofi Annan - Interventions` | `Thomas Weiss` | `Responsibility to Protect (R2P)` | `Veto Limitation` | `Syrian Conflict Gridlock` | `Transparency in Budget` | `Peacekeeping Funding` | `Mali and CAR Challenges`

### ğŸ“ Answer Structure

**Introduction**
- Need for UN structural and functional reforms widely recognized
- Organization struggles with contemporary global challenges
- Founded 1945: framework designed to prevent another world war
- Decision-making power centered in Security Council's P5
- P5: US, UK, France, Russia, China
- Post-WWII structure fails to reflect current geopolitical realities
- Kofi Annan ("Interventions: A Life in War and Peace"): "UN created to solve problems no single country could resolve alone"
- Inefficiencies and imbalances hinder this mission
- Particularly in peacekeeping and conflict resolution
- Reform imperative for 21st-century relevance

**Body**

*Security Council Composition Reform*
- P5 holding veto power criticized as outdated and unrepresentative
- Emerging powers advocate for permanent seats
- India, Brazil, Germany, Japan (G4 nations)
- Reflect economic and regional influence
- India: represents nearly one-fifth world's population
- Significant contributor to UN peacekeeping missions
- Yet lacks permanent seat
- Thomas Weiss ("What's Wrong with the United Nations and How to Fix It"): without reforms
- UN risks losing legitimacy as representative global body
- Current composition reflects WWII power structure
- Not contemporary multipolar reality
- Africa entirely unrepresented in permanent membership
- Latin America and Asia underrepresented
- Need for geographical and demographic balance

*Decision-Making Process Reform*
- Veto power allows any P5 member to block resolutions
- Often leads to political gridlock
- Syrian conflict: repeated vetoes by Russia and China
- Hampered decisive action on humanitarian crisis
- Undermines UN's ability to respond
- Suggested reform: limit veto use in cases of mass atrocities
- Supported by "Responsibility to Protect" (R2P) doctrine
- Aimed at preventing genocide and war crimes
- Could prevent humanitarian paralysis
- When urgent action needed
- Procedural reforms to enhance Council efficiency
- Regular review of veto exercise
- Requirement to justify veto publicly

*Administrative Efficiency and Accountability*
- UN bureaucracy criticized for slow response times
- Financial inefficiencies widespread
- Institute greater transparency in budget allocations
- Create mechanisms to track performance
- Could streamline operations
- Decentralize decision-making for faster responses
- Reduce bureaucratic layers
- Empower field offices with autonomy
- Current system: over-centralized in New York
- Delays critical decisions in conflict zones

*Peacekeeping Forces Enhancement*
- Need better funding and training
- Address complex modern conflicts
- Challenges in African regions: Mali, Central African Republic
- Peacekeepers often under-resourced
- Lack adequate training for civilian protection
- Mandate clarity issues
- Pre-deployment training standardization needed
- Better equipment and logistical support
- Stronger collaboration with regional organizations (AU, ECOWAS)
- Protection of civilians doctrine strengthening

*Financial Structure Reform*
- Over-dependence on few major donors creates imbalances
- Diversify funding sources
- Assess contributions based on updated economic indicators
- Reduce vulnerability to funding withdrawals
- Ensure financial sustainability
- Transparent financial reporting mechanisms

**Conclusion**
- UN requires structural reforms, particularly Security Council
- Functional improvements in decision-making and administration essential
- Expanding representation to include emerging powers
- Limiting veto power in humanitarian crises
- Enhancing transparency and efficiency
- UN could better fulfill 21st-century mission
- Address global challenges more effectively
- Maintain relevance in changing world order
- Balance representation with operational effectiveness

## <a id="q12"></a>Q12. What are the real objectives of Millennium Development Goals (MDG)? Do you think that MDGs have been able to achieve the desired goals of poverty alleviation and sustainable development? (2015) (15 Marks)

### ğŸ¯ Core Theme
The MDGs (2000-2015) established eight objectives to address critical human development issues, achieving notable poverty reduction success (halved global poverty rates) primarily in East Asia, but faced challenges in Sub-Saharan Africa and sustainability targets, revealing need for localized strategies and comprehensive approaches beyond economic indicators.

### ğŸ“ Answer Structure

**Introduction**
- MDGs established 2000 by United Nations
- Designed as global framework for critical human development
- Improve living standards worldwide
- Eight core objectives guided MDGs:
  1. Eradicate extreme poverty and hunger
  2. Achieve universal primary education
  3. Promote gender equality
  4. Reduce child mortality
  5. Improve maternal health
  6. Combat HIV/AIDS, malaria, other diseases
  7. Ensure environmental sustainability
  8. Develop global partnership for development
- Kofi Annan: MDGs as "blueprint agreed by all world's countries and leading development institutions"
- Address critical issues by 2015
- Primary focus: poverty reduction
- Objective: halve proportion living on less than $1.25/day

**Body**

*Progress in Poverty Alleviation*
- MDGs made significant progress
- Also faced challenges in fully realizing ambitions
- UN data: global poverty rates fell by more than half
- Between 1990 and 2015
- Notable achievement primarily attributed to economic growth
- East Asia and the Pacific led reductions
- China's economic transformation major factor
- Lifted hundreds of millions from extreme poverty
- Success demonstrated achievability with right policies
- Economic growth combined with targeted interventions effective

*Regional Disparities in Achievement*
- Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia lagged
- Millions still living in poverty despite global progress
- Due to political instability
- Inadequate infrastructure
- Weak governance structures
- Armed conflicts disrupting development
- Jeffrey Sachs: MDGs mobilized unprecedented global efforts
- Structural inequalities hindered equitable progress
- Limited funding in certain regions
- Need for more localized strategies
- One-size-fits-all approach insufficient
- Context-specific interventions necessary

*Sustainable Development: Mixed Results*
- Led to increased access to clean water
- Over two billion people gained access
- Spurred environmental awareness globally
- However, environmental targets fell short
- Continued deforestation persisted
- Greenhouse gas emissions rose globally
- Global emissions target missed
- Carbon dioxide emissions increased
- Challenge balancing development with environmental sustainability
- Economic growth often prioritized over environmental protection

*Critique of MDG Framework*
- William Easterly critique: MDGs set ambitious universal goals
- Without fully accounting for regional disparities
- Often prioritized economic indicators
- Over long-term sustainability measures
- Top-down approach rather than participatory
- Limited country ownership in some cases
- Funding mechanisms inadequate
- Measurement challenges for some targets
- Overlooked inequality within countries
- Focused on aggregate national data

*Legacy and Transition to SDGs*
- MDGs created momentum for future frameworks
- Transitioned to Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) in 2015
- SDGs more comprehensive: 17 goals vs 8 MDGs
- Lessons learned informed SDG design
- Greater emphasis on environmental sustainability
- Focus on reducing inequalities
- Universal application (developed and developing countries)
- Participatory approach in goal-setting

**Conclusion**
- MDGs catalyzed substantial advancements in poverty reduction
- Created momentum for sustainable development frameworks
- Gaps remain in comprehensive poverty alleviation
- Sustainable development not fully achieved
- Legacy is mixed: successes and shortcomings
- Succeeded in raising awareness
- Focusing resources on crucial issues
- Demonstrated tackling global poverty requires adaptable strategies
- Locally-responsive approaches essential
- Context-sensitive implementation needed
- Foundation for more comprehensive SDG framework
