### ğŸ“ Answer Structure

**Introduction**
- ICJ established 1945 under UN Charter
- Principal judicial organ of United Nations

- Dual mandate: settle state disputes and give legal opinions
- Malcolm Shaw ("International Law" 2008): ICJ fosters international legal standards
- Promotes peaceful dispute resolution

**Body**

*Structure of the ICJ*
- Defined by its statute (integral part of UN Charter)
- Composed of 15 judges
- Elected by UN General Assembly and Security Council
- Nine-year terms with option for re-election
- Chosen based on competence in international law
- Representative of world's principal legal systems
- Ensures diversity and fairness
- No two judges from same country
- Five judges elected every three years (maintains continuity)
- Ad hoc judges provision: when party lacks national representation
- Ensures fair representation of all states' interests

*Functions: Contentious Jurisdiction*
- Adjudicates legal disputes submitted by states
- Participation voluntary but binding once consent given
- Notable case: Nicaragua v. United States (1986)
- Ruled on U.S. intervention in Nicaragua
- Reinforced principles of state sovereignty
- Demonstrates capacity to hold powerful nations accountable
- Jurisdiction requires state consent to proceed
- Binding nature of judgments once accepted

*Functions: Advisory Jurisdiction*
- Provides legal opinions at request of UN organs
- Specialized agencies can request opinions
- These opinions are non-binding
- Significant case: Legality of separation wall in Occupied Palestinian Territory (2004)
- Illustrates ICJ's role in shaping international opinion
- Addresses complex legal and moral issues
- Influences global discourse on contentious matters

*Symbolic and Practical Roles*
- Provides legal avenue for peaceful dispute resolution
- Contributes to development of international law
- Creates legal precedent
- Encourages international norms
- Enforcement relies on state compliance
- UN Security Council can enforce in some cases
- Influence through moral authority and legal reasoning

**Conclusion**
- ICJ structure: diverse bench with fairness provisions
- Supports mission of dispute resolution and advisory opinions
- Reinforces international law framework
- Impact depends on political will of states
- Effectiveness contingent on respect and implementation of judgments
- Despite limitations, essential for global legal order
- Promotes rule of law in international relations

### ğŸ¯ Core Theme

### ğŸ“ Answer Structure

**Introduction**
- UNSC: one of six principal UN organs
- Primarily tasked with maintaining international peace and security
- Established 1945 under UN Charter
- Structure reflects post-WWII geopolitical realities
- Allied powers sought mechanism to prevent future global conflicts
- 15 members: 5 permanent (P5) with veto power
- 10 non-permanent elected for two-year terms (regional representation)
- Inis Claude: UNSC as "power politics meets idealism"

**Body**

*Structure and Composition*
- Five permanent members (P5): US, UK, Russia, China, France
- Each holds veto power over substantive resolutions
- Ten non-permanent members elected by UN General Assembly
- Two-year terms without immediate re-election
- Based on equitable geographical distribution
- Regional representation ensures diverse perspectives
- P5 reflects WWII victors' continued dominance
- Composition often criticized as outdated

*Primary Functions: Conflict Resolution*
- Article 24: "primary responsibility" for international peace
- Chapter VI: investigate disputes, recommend peaceful settlement
- Powers to respond to threats or breaches of peace
- Israeli-Palestinian dispute: UNSC resolution efforts
- Facilitation of negotiation (varying success levels)
- Diplomatic intervention and mediation
- Preventive diplomacy initiatives

*Enforcement and Peacekeeping Powers*
- Chapter VII: broader powers for enforcement
- Can impose economic and diplomatic sanctions
- Authorize use of military force
- Deploy peacekeeping operations
- North Korea and Iran sanctions: nuclear proliferation curtailment
- Demonstrates ability to enforce international norms compliance
- UN Mission in South Sudan (UNMISS): peacekeeping example
- Commitment to stabilizing conflict zones
- Non-combat forces protect civilians, monitor peace processes
- Challenges: limited resources, complex conflict dynamics

*Role in Global Governance and Norm-Setting*
- Passes binding resolutions on member states
- Establishes norms on counter-terrorism, non-proliferation
- Resolution 1373 (2001): post-9/11 counter-terrorism measures
- Influenced national laws worldwide
- Creates international legal obligations
- Shapes global security architecture

*Structural Limitations*
- P5 veto power creates frequent gridlock
- Limits effectiveness when P5 interests clash
- Syrian conflict: multiple vetoes by Russia and China
- Prevented unified humanitarian action
- Calls for reform to address power imbalance
- Need for more representative decision-making

**Conclusion**
- UNSC structure grants significant authority in global security
- Functionality constrained by P5 interests
- Effective in peacekeeping and norm-setting
- Structural limitations reveal need for reform
- Veto power reliance reduces responsiveness
- Must adapt to complex modern security challenges
- Balance needed between power and representation

## <a id="q3"></a>Q3. Describe the composition of the International Court of Justice (ICJ). Discuss its voluntary jurisdiction. (2022) (15 Marks)

### ğŸ¯ Core Theme
The ICJ's 15-judge composition ensures global legal diversity and impartiality, but its voluntary jurisdiction principleâ€”requiring state consent for contentious casesâ€”fundamentally limits its authority, as powerful states can refuse participation or reject unfavorable judgments.

### ğŸ”‘ Key Recall Terms
`15 Judges - Global Representation` | `9-Year Staggered Terms` | `GA and SC Election` | `Major Legal Systems` | `No Two Judges Same State` | `Voluntary Jurisdiction` | `Contentious vs Advisory` | `Article 36 ICJ Statute` | `Optional Clause` | `State Consent Required` | `Nicaragua v. US (1986)` | `US Withdrawal` | `Compulsory Jurisdiction Limits` | `Treaty Clauses` | `Special Agreements` | `Geopolitical Constraints`

### ğŸ“ Answer Structure

**Introduction**
- ICJ established 1945 as principal judicial organ of UN
- Composed of 15 judges
- Elected by UN General Assembly and Security Council
- Nine-year terms
- Chosen based on international law qualifications
- Reflect world's major legal systems and geographical diversity
- No two judges may be nationals of same state
- Ensures broader representation
- Composition emphasizes impartiality and global inclusiveness
- Critical for maintaining credibility in adjudicating disputes

**Body**

*Composition Details and Election Process*
- Elections occur every three years
- One-third of seats up for election each time
- Ensures continuity in Court's work
- Judge may be re-elected
- Can serve on cases even after term expires if initially part of deliberation
- Ad hoc judges provision for unrepresented parties
- If party lacks judge of its nationality on bench
- Allows each disputing party to feel represented
- Potentially enhances fairness and acceptance of decisions
- Promotes confidence in judicial process

*Jurisdiction Categories*
- Two types: contentious and advisory
- Contentious: settles disputes between states
- Advisory: provides legal opinions to UN organs
- Contentious jurisdiction limited by voluntary principle
- Court can only adjudicate with state consent
- Distinguishes ICJ from domestic courts with mandatory jurisdiction

*Voluntary Jurisdiction: Three Pathways (Article 36)*
- **First pathway**: Treaties or conventions with ICJ resolution clause
- Bilateral or multilateral agreements specifying ICJ
- **Second pathway**: Special agreement for specific case
- Both parties consent to submit particular dispute
- **Third pathway**: Unilateral declaration accepting compulsory jurisdiction
- Known as "optional clause" under Article 36(2)
- States can accept ICJ jurisdiction without case-specific agreements
- Subject to reservations limiting reach
- Many states add conditions or exclude certain matters

*Constraints of Voluntary Jurisdiction*
- Often constrains ICJ effectiveness
- States can refuse to submit to authority
- May fail to comply with judgments
- Nicaragua v. United States (1986): paradigmatic example
- ICJ ruled in favor of Nicaragua
- Found US intervention violated international law
- US rejected judgment
- Withdrew acceptance of compulsory jurisdiction
- Exemplifies challenges where powerful states evade rulings
- Reduces Court's enforcement capacity
- Affects role in upholding international law
- Geopolitical interests often override legal obligations

*Impact on ICJ Authority*
- Without mandatory jurisdiction, effectiveness depends on cooperation
- Challenging when geopolitical interests at stake
- States may "forum shop" or avoid ICJ entirely
- Limits Court's ability to develop consistent jurisprudence
- Creates gaps in international legal accountability
- Powerful nations can act with relative impunity

**Conclusion**
- ICJ composition strives for global representation and neutrality
- Voluntary jurisdiction poses fundamental limitations to authority
- Without mandatory jurisdiction, depends on state cooperation
- Particularly difficult when national interests conflict
- Court remains vital institution for promoting international legal principles
- Relies on states' commitment to peaceful dispute resolution
- Moral authority exists despite enforcement limitations
- Continues to contribute to international law development
